## 📋 Abstract
This research provides an in-depth analytical study of one of contemporary physics' greatest enigmas: the symmetry between matter and antimatter[cite: 1]. The paper begins by investigating the historical background of antimatter, progressing toward a modern understanding of its complex physical properties, including charge conjugation and CPT symmetry[cite: 1]. The central problem addressed in this study is the "Baryon Asymmetry" resulting from the Big Bang, which raises fundamental questions regarding the absence of antimatter in the observable universe despite theoretical predictions suggesting equal creation of both matter and antimatter[cite: 1].

The methodology employed in this research involves an analytical review of landmark scientific experiments conducted at the European Organization for Nuclear Research (CERN)[cite: 1]. Specifically, the study evaluates the significance of experiments such as ALPHA, CMS, ATLAS, and BASE, assessing the precision of antihydrogen spectral measurements and particle confinement results[cite: 1]. Furthermore, the research transcends theoretical boundaries to analyze the practical future applications of antimatter in deep-space propulsion, energy generation, and nuclear medicine[cite: 1]. The research concludes by offering a comprehensive perspective that bridges experimental laboratory data with cosmological phenomena, contributing to a deeper understanding of the universe's origin and the evolution of matter[cite: 1].

## ⚠️ Research Problem
Antimatter represents one of the most prominent and complex dilemmas in modern physics[cite: 1]. It serves as the opposite mirror to ordinary matter, sharing identical physical properties, such as mass, but exhibiting entirely reversed quantum properties (e.g., electric charge)[cite: 1]. However, the fundamental problem emerges in astrophysics and cosmology, specifically concerning the origin of the universe[cite: 1]. According to the Standard Model of particle physics, in the initial moments following the Big Bang, the early universe should have generated exactly equal amounts of matter and antimatter[cite: 1].

The physical paradox lies in the phenomenon of their interaction; when matter and antimatter collide, they completely annihilate each other (Annihilation), leaving behind only pure energy[cite: 1]. Had the universe begun with perfect symmetry, all matter would have been annihilated, preventing the formation of the cosmos as we know it—including stars, galaxies, and human beings[cite: 1]. The mysterious imbalance that allowed ordinary matter to survive by a tiny fraction and form the observable universe is known in physics as "Baryon Asymmetry," which constitutes the core problem investigated in this research[cite: 1].
